📖 Story Summary

On The Face Of It

A detailed summary of Susan Hill's poignant one-act play — exploring isolation, friendship, and the courage to embrace life fully.

🌿 Background & Context

On the Face of It is a one-act play written by Susan Hill, included in the CBSE Class 12 English supplementary reader Vistas. The play is a dialogue-driven exploration of two physically disabled individuals who find unexpected friendship and meaning in a walled garden.

The title itself is deeply ironic — "on the face of it" suggests judging things by their surface appearance, which is exactly what society does to both protagonists, and exactly what the play urges us not to do.

🧒 Part I — Derry Enters the Garden

The play opens with Derry, a fourteen-year-old boy, climbing over the wall into Mr. Lamb's garden. He believes the garden is empty and wants to be alone. Derry has a badly scarred face — half of it burned by acid. Society's cruelty has made him bitter and withdrawn; he avoids people because of the way they stare, whisper, or recoil from him.

Mr. Lamb, an old man who walks with a tin leg (having lost his leg in the war), discovers Derry in his garden but does not shoo him away. Instead, he welcomes him warmly and begins to talk. Derry is suspicious and defensive at first — he expects the same pity or horror he receives from everyone else.

Mr. Lamb keeps his gate open always, saying that anyone is welcome. Derry is puzzled by this old man who seems genuinely unbothered by Derry's appearance. Mr. Lamb points out that Derry's heart, hands, and mind all work perfectly — that his face is just a face.

👴 Part II — Mr. Lamb's Philosophy

As the conversation deepens, Mr. Lamb reveals his own wisdom about life. He speaks about how he loves all things equally — bees, weeds, crab apples, children, old ladies — everything that comes into his garden is welcome. He collects tins of crab apples to make jelly. He reads his books in the sun. He is a man who has chosen to live fully despite his disability.

He tells Derry about a man who locked himself in a room to protect himself from the world — and was eventually crushed by a picture falling off the wall. The lesson: you cannot hide from life. The world will find you. Better to live in it openly.

Derry is deeply moved but also conflicted. He admits that he hates being looked at, hates the things people say about him. Mr. Lamb listens without pity — he challenges Derry not with sympathy but with perspective. He says that other people's words only have the power Derry gives them.

🏠 Part III — Derry's Return Home & Coming Back

Derry goes home to tell his mother he wants to return to Mr. Lamb's garden. His mother tries to dissuade him — she is overprotective and warns him that the old man is "different" and that people talk about him. But for the first time, Derry resists her. He insists on going back.

His mother warns him about Mr. Lamb, calling him odd. But Derry has found something in this old man that no one else has given him — he has been treated as a full person, not as a damaged one. He tells her, "He's the only person who's ever talked to me like... like it didn't matter."

Derry rushes back to the garden, determined and full of new energy. This is the most significant change in his character — he is choosing connection over isolation for the first time.

💔 Part IV — The Tragic Ending

When Derry returns to the garden, he finds Mr. Lamb has fallen from a ladder while trying to pick crab apples from the tree — the very tree he loved. Mr. Lamb lies dead in his garden.

The ending is deeply ironic: the man who preached living fully and embracing life has died doing exactly that — living. He did not lock himself away. He was not killed by hiding. He died engaged with the world he loved.

Derry kneels beside Mr. Lamb and weeps. This is a profound moment — Derry, who claimed not to cry, who claimed to feel nothing, is overwhelmed by grief. It shows how deeply Mr. Lamb had already changed him. Even in death, Mr. Lamb's influence is alive in Derry.

🎭 Major Themes

  • Physical disability and social prejudice
  • Isolation vs. human connection
  • Acceptance and self-worth
  • The philosophy of living fully
  • Death and the meaning of life
  • Overprotection vs. independence

🌟 Key Symbols

  • The Garden — openness, inclusivity, nature
  • The Wall — isolation, separation from society
  • Crab Apples — things dismissed as useless
  • Tin Leg / Scarred Face — visible disability
  • The Open Gate — welcome to all
  • The Ladder — risk, ambition, living

👥 Character Analysis

  • Derry: bitter → hopeful → transformed
  • Mr. Lamb: wise, optimistic, mentor figure
  • Derry's Mother: love expressed as overprotection
  • Both protagonists: physically different, human
  • Society: represented through what others say

✍️ Literary Devices

  • Irony: title and ending
  • Symbolism throughout the garden
  • Contrast: Derry vs Mr. Lamb
  • Dialogue as primary narrative tool
  • Anecdote: the man in the locked room
  • Foreshadowing: crab apple tree

💡 Important Quotes

"There's nothing wrong with your face. It's a face. Just a face." — Mr. Lamb to Derry
"He said I could... he said it didn't matter, what I looked like, or what I was." — Derry to his mother
"Weeds are plants. The gate's always open." — Mr. Lamb
